Fruition

Summary

Complex prescription apps are contributing to epidemic levels of non-adherence. Sphere was designed with a goal of seeing a measurable increase in adherence and improvement of patient health.

Over the years I’ve had my share of poor experiences that resulted in missing medication. Misses which impacted tests months down the road. In early 2018 I took this on as a personal project to explore solutions that could repair the broken pharmacy experience.

Problem

Over 50% of patients do not take their prescriptions, and 30% fail to fill them at all. Resulting in 30%-50% of treatment failures, hospitalization, and 125,000 deaths per year.

Solution

Use of mobile pill reminders improves adherence by a minimum of 12.3%. An efficient and transparent pharmacy results in an additional increase in adherence.

Competitive Analysis

In addition to a couple loan servicers above, I conducted a detailed analysis on direct competitors Student Loan Hero (recently bought by Lendedu and discontinued their dashboard), Unbury.me, Tuition.io, and Intuit Mint. In essence, I found most products in this space are bloated or underdeveloped. In particular, there were two findings that stood out to me:

1

Business Model - Product is tangled with it’s business model (e.g. ad-based) causing a cluttered dashboard or limiting itself through exclusive partnerships with employers.

2

Motivations - Heavily influenced by underlying motivations (e.g. loan servicers driven by commissions and interest payments).

Demographic

According to data gathered by the Federal Reserve in 2016, adoption of financial apps is highest by a young educated demographic. I decided my approach would be to gain users by partnering primarily with colleges. To dig deeper into that approach I answered two questions:

  • What state had the highest average and proportion of debt?
  • What schools in that state were highest in those two categories?

It turned out that Pennsylvania is ranked number one in both categories. The highest ranked colleges in Pennsylvania are Lincoln University, Penn State Hazleton, and Penn State Greater Allegheny.

User stories and flows

I created seven user flows and a sitemap of the product that you can see in the link below. The main flows included onboarding, selecting a loan status, counselor chat, adding a student loan, and a roadmap with tools.

The direction research took me would ultimately require a much larger team of counselors, writers, and finance professionals to make the experience what it needs to be. I understood my job would be to create a reusable and simple design that handled content well and could be swapped in and out easily.

Sketching and low-fidelity

Prior to sketching I lead with content and thought about a mobile-first or desktop-first approach. The mobile-first approach is typically my preferred method, but the limitations it posed for core features and roadmap checkpoints in particular would render major parts of the product almost unusable on mobile.

I wasn’t convinced with a relatively short timeframe and as the only designer that mobile-first was the right approach in this situation. I prioritized desktop for the MVP while working on mobile at the same time. Replicating the desktop experience as much as possible.

Usability Testing

I gathered feedback from potential users on the lofi pill reminders, onboarding, and main navigation screens. Finding a need for:

  • Foundation - app relies on trust, timing, and managing expectations.
  • Reminder details - frequency (daily/weekly), times per day, units (e.g.tablets capsules), and ability to mark as taken from their lock screen.
  • Security - how their personal and medical information is being protected. Compliant with HIPPA
  • Clear communication - when pharmacists are available for live chat and what their camera is being used for.

High Fidelity Mockups

The 1-on-1 counseling feature is a core part of Fruition, however, the two most visited pages would be the dashboard and landing page. I used preference testing, design critiques, and usability tests to guide the interface of these screens and then allow it to filter into everything else. Creating a consistent and cohesive product.

Mobile Challenges

Creating mobile designs at the same time as desktop and iterating was insanely difficult. It got out of hand quickly and often. I tried to manage it by keeping designs relatively close to where desktop was.

I finally brought the landing page and desktop up to speed, but the rest of mobile was grayscale, inconsistent, and frankly old designs. Luckily, the difficulties on mobile were eased by the thoroughness of my work on desktop. Allowing mobile to take shape as the MVP was nearing completion.

Usability testing & iterations

With little time left, the goal was ship “good-enough” flows and not perfection. Making sure users could complete tasks with as little hiccups as possible and then launch, test, and iterate more accurately.

Unhappy states and blankstates

Throughout the project I made sure I was designing for empty states and error states. Backed by research like the optimal placement for imput feedback in responsive applications.

Logo Design

The branding for Fruition came later in the process of this project. I started sketching ideas related to students and the loan process, but struggled with its form. After some thorough analog and digital sketching I landed on a graduation cap made out of two F’s. Reusing the product typography for its wordmark. I put together a style guide detailing its proper use and other aspects of the Fruition system.

Conclusion

I learned so much over the course of this project and especially about mobile-first design. In my decision to design desktop-first I had the opportunity to understand where many companies stand today. Faced with scaling down their products and tackling the difficult challenges that come with that. It ultimately reaffirmed my preference for mobile-first, but the design process can be messy. Limited by timeframe, resources, and more requires you to adapt efficiently and produce quality work for users and the business.

I also learned a lot about managing scope. The path my research led me to made it so easy to overthink and allow the project to get away from me. I kept coming back to saying no to uneccesary features and continued figuring out the core of the product. This process made me realize qualities about myself as a designer. My ability to refine, problem solve, work through huge project dips, and utilize other designers and user testing to find my out of challenges.

Lastly, as a designer who is detail oriented, reaching a point of “good enough” was a huge area of growth for me. There’s always more that can be improved, but it’s key to understand when a product is good enough and launch on-time and within budget.
